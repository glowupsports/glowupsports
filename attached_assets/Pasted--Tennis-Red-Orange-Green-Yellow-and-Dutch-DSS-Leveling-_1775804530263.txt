## Scope, terminology, and how “levels” were interpreted

### What “Red/Orange/Green/Yellow” means in public federation materials  
Most federations treat **Red, Orange, Green** as **scaled tennis stages** for juniors (typically up to ~10–12 years old), defined primarily by **environment constraints** (court size, ball compression, net height) and **appropriate match formats**, not by a universal skill test. citeturn5search8turn5search11turn23view1turn19search1  

“Yellow” is generally shorthand for **full-court standard tennis ball play** (the regular ball/court), and is often the “next step after Green.” citeturn23view0turn21view1turn15search3  

### What “DSS 9–1” means in the Netherlands  
In Dutch tennis, **DSS** refers to the **KNLTB** rating system that updates a player’s **rating after each official match** by formula, and then assigns an annual **speelsterkte (1–9)** derived from rating boundaries. citeturn25view0turn16view0turn7view0  

Crucially, **DSS is not a technique exam**; it is a **competition-performance classification**. Any “technical requirements per DSS level” are therefore either:
- Not publicly specified (officially “unspecified”), or  
- A **coaching translation** (a practical expectations profile that *predicts* what players at that DSS typically can do). citeturn25view0turn16view0turn7view0  

### How this report handles your requested Red/Orange/Green/Yellow 3–2–1 breakdown  
You requested **Red 3/2/1; Orange 3/2/1; Green 3/2/1; Yellow 3/2/1**. Public federation materials vary:

- **USTA Net Generation** publishes **Red 03/02/01 and Orange 03/02/01**, but **Green is 02/01 (two levels)** and **Yellow is 2/1 (two levels)** in the publicly available practice plans. citeturn12view0turn11view0turn13view0turn21view0turn21view1

## Dutch DSS: what it measures and how to translate it into coaching requirements

### What DSS and speelsterkte actually are (officially)

The Dutch federation’s public explanation is explicit:

- Every registered player receives an integer **speelsterkte** and a decimal **rating** (separate for singles and doubles). citeturn25view0turn16view0  
- Speelsterkte runs **1–9**, where **9 is a beginning player** and **1 is a professional** (and is used for competition lineup and tournament entries). citeturn25view0  
- The decimal rating is updated after each official match; the yearly speelsterkte is determined at season end from rating boundaries. citeturn25view0turn7view0  
- The DSS algorithm is published: it computes a win expectation from ratings and then updates ratings with a maximum step size **K = 0.275** per match (also stated by KNLTB’s FAQ). citeturn16view0turn25view1turn25view2  

**Key technical clarity:** In the DSS formula example, the winner’s numeric rating can move **down** (improving), meaning the system’s “better” direction is toward smaller rating numbers. citeturn16view2turn16view3  

### DSS 9–1 rating boundaries (official 2026 procedure)

KNLTB publishes yearly boundaries for converting end-of-season ratings into speelsterkte; for 2026 (tennis) the published table includes explicit rating cutoffs for many categories plus ranking-based slots for top tiers. citeturn7view0  

A high-level interpretation from the published 2026 procedure:
- Speelsterkte 1 and (part of) 2 are assigned by **KNLTB ranking list positions** (e.g., top positions). citeturn7view0  
- Speelsterkte 2–8 include numeric rating boundaries (e.g., 2 at around ~2.49 / ~2.76 depending on discipline), and 9 is for “all other players with rating from 9.0000” (with additional conditions noted). citeturn7view0  

Because these boundaries are **season-specific**, your app should treat this as a **versioned configuration** (e.g., “KNLTB 2026 conversion table”). citeturn7view0  

### Technical/performance requirements for DSS 9–1

**Officially:** the Dutch federation materials retrieved here do not define “required stroke lists, stances, combinations” per speelsterkte as a public exam rubric. As a result, the “technical requirements” for DSS levels are **Unspecified (publicly)**. citeturn25view0turn7view0turn16view0  

**Practically (coaching translation you can use):** DSS is match-results-derived, so the most defensible way to define “DSS technical requirements” inside your product is to define **observable match KPIs that predict rating improvement**, then map those KPIs to skills+drills. ### Common technical errors and corrective exercises (high-yield set)

This section is designed as “coach scripting”: error → correction cue → constraint drill.

**Serve toss + service motion**
- **Error:** toss behind head → contact collapses, back arches, double faults rise.  
- **Corrective:** enforce “toss arm leads” and contact “in front” cues; use shadow swings + target tosses; integrate serve + second-ball recovery early (HTO explicitly recommends practicing serve combined with the next ball to fix late recovery). citeturn23view3turn11view1  

**Forehand spacing and contact**
- **Error:** jammed contact beside body; ball sprays.  
- **Corrective:** “create space” with adjustment steps; cooperative rally with a spacing constraint (must contact outside front hip). Competency rubrics repeatedly emphasize contact in front and non-dominant hand support during unit turn. citeturn11view0turn11view1turn12view2  

**Backhand stability**
- **Error:** “push-only” backhand; no acceleration; breaks under pace.  
- **Corrective:** groove unit turn + stable grip choice; progress from fed balls to live ball; use crosscourt-only games to remove line-risk. citeturn12view2turn11view0  

**Split-step timing**
- **Error:** split-step missing or too early; late first step.  
- **Corrective:** “land as opponent contacts” cue; build in callout timing drills; this aligns with advanced competency descriptors and research that studies split-step timing differences across levels. citeturn13view0turn15search27  

**Transition and volleys**
- **Error:** swinging volleys; contact too close; net clearance errors.  
- **Corrective:** blocking volley with racquet head above wrist; approach + volley sequences from feed (explicitly in competencies). citeturn12view2turn11view0turn11view1
