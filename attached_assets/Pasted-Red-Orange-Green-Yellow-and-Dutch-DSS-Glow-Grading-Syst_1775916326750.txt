Red–Orange–Green–Yellow and Dutch DSS/Glow Grading Systems
Executive summary
This research found that most national tennis governing bodies publicly define Red/Orange/Green/Yellow primarily as scaled environments and competition formats, not as a universal “belt exam” with stroke-by-stroke pass/fail gates. The major exception in publicly available materials is the United States Tennis Association (USTA), which publishes explicit competencies for Red Ball 03/02/01, Orange Ball 03/02/01, and Green Ball 02/01, plus assessment forms and a coach assessment guide with a published pass threshold (85%) for certain level transitions. 

For the Netherlands, the Dynamische Speelsterkte Systeem (DSS) is an official, published match-results-based rating system used to assign your annual integer speelsterkte 1–9 (9 = beginner; 1 = professional). DSS provides the math and yearly boundaries, but does not publish a technique curriculum as “requirements per DSS level.” 

Your Glow adult/performance track (Glow 9→Glow 1) is therefore structurally closer to DSS than to junior stage programs: it is defined by MMR/Elo outcomes with skill gates, where the app decides rank (user-provided). Because you requested “more in depth,” this report expands Glow 9–1 into a full technical + performance rubric while clearly labeling what is User‑provided vs. Analytical (derived).

A key practical conclusion: if you want Red3/2/1 → Orange3/2/1 → Green3/2/1 → Yellow3/2/1 across multiple federations, you must treat USTA’s published competencies as one of the few “true” public micro-level datasets, while for other federations you must mark sub-level criteria as “Unspecified (publicly)” and/or adopt a derived belt standard that is explicitly your curriculum (mapped to each federation’s stage rules and match formats).

How federations define the stages (why “sublevels” are often not official)
Across national bodies, the consistent public standard is that Red/Orange/Green are scaled tennis stages (court + ball + racquet + match format), designed so players can “serve, rally, and score” early. 

The international standard (Tennis10s) treats Red/Orange/Green as three stages for 10-and-under, with court/ball/racquet scaling and recommended shorter formats. 
The Netherlands (Tenniskids) treats Red/Orange/Green as program phases up to age 12 and provides detailed scoring formats; it does not publish a public “Red 3/2/1” technique exam, and from 2026 it groups competition by TOF progress rather than fixed sublevels. 
The USTA is an outlier in publishing explicit 03/02/01 competency lists and assessment sheets.

Common failure points in junior transitions
Because most federations do not publish “failure reasons,” the most defensible approach is to identify common failure points from published competencies (especially USTA) and from stage logic (scaled tennis). The recurring patterns are consistent across programs:

Tempo jumps before motor control: players move to a faster ball/court when they can’t yet control depth and height, leading to short rallies and low “playability.” Tennis10s explicitly cites this motivation/retention problem if kids are pushed too early into Yellow-ball full-court play. 
Serve and return collapse under scoring: higher levels expect routines and placement (e.g., wide/body/T in Green 01 and Orange 01). People often “have the stroke” but can’t reproduce it under point pressure. 
Footwork timing problems (split-step): competency progressions explicitly add “split-step on opponent contact” and later “dynamic split-step slightly after opponent contact.”

Dutch DSS: what is officially specified
DSS is a results-based rating system designed to group players into competitive strength bands (speelsterkte 1–9) using a published formula based on win expectation. 

Key official points that affect how you should define “requirements”:

Speelsterkte is an integer from 1 to 9; 9 is assigned to a beginning player and 1 to a professional player. 
Player ratings update after each match using a published Elo-like approach:
Formula uses a win expectation (“Prob”) and a maximum step K = 0.275; a worked example is published. 
The 2026 boundaries for converting end-of-season rating to speelsterkte are published (e.g., rating cutoffs for 2–8; and ranking-list assignment for the top banded tiers). 
What DSS does not publicly specify: a technique test (“required shots per DSS level”) is not defined as an official public curriculum requirement in the DSS documentation retrieved. Therefore, any “technical DSS 9→1 requirements” are Unspecified (publicly) and must be treated as coaching translations (see below).

Weekly training plan templates
These templates are designed to satisfy: (a) skill acquisition, (b) match volume for rating systems (Glow/DSS), and (c) measurable milestones.

Template for juniors (Red→Yellow): 3 sessions + 1 play day (minimum)

Session A (60–75 min): rally control + movement (split-step, recover) + point-play games (serve-rally-score). The “serve, rally, score” design is central to Tennis10s and Tennis Canada’s point-play curriculum. 
Session B (60–75 min): serve/return fundamentals + transition (approach/volley, overhead) + short competitive formats (tie-breaks/short sets). 
Session C (60–75 min): tactical theme (cross→open; depth as pressure) + games to targets (“safelines”), aligned with tactical fundamentals like zones and depth. 
Play day: use stage-appropriate official scoring (Tenniskids rules or Tennis10s short formats). 
Template for adults (Glow/DSS): 2 training sessions + 2 match days

Training 1 (75–90 min): serve+1 and return+1 patterns; end with 20 minutes of pressure games (tiebreaks). Pattern language aligns with USTA Orange/Green competencies (serve+1, attack weak second serve). 
Training 2 (75–90 min): defense-to-neutral + transition to net; include split-step video-tagging. Split-step timing is a documented differentiator across playing levels. 
Match day 1 + 2: log verified matches (best-of formats or sets). This supports rating stability in DSS-like systems whose updates are match driven. 
Monthly testing: ITF fitness testing protocols for measurable standards. 
Common technical errors and corrective exercises
This table is intended for coaches and for building “failure point” explanations inside your app.

Error pattern	Corrective cue	Corrective drill
Late preparation on groundstrokes (backswing not complete by bounce)	“Prepare by the bounce.”	Tennis Australia explicitly highlights early preparation within stage fundamentals; use feed → rally with a “bounce prep” rule. 
Inconsistent contact point (jammed, hit beside body)	“Create space with adjustment steps; contact in front.”	Red/Orange/Green competencies emphasize spacing (“creates space using adjustment steps,” “contact in front”). Do cooperative rally; point only counts if contact occurs in front hip zone. 
Split-step missing or mistimed	“Land on (or slightly after) opponent contact.”	USTA Orange 01 and Green 01/02 specify split-step timing; run 2‑minute video-tag segments and score presence rate. 
Second serve collapses under pressure	“Spin + height first; target middle.”	Green 01 requires 2nd serve depth+spin; use 20‑serve ladder: only count serves landing beyond service line depth zone. 
Approaching on the wrong ball (approach without pressure)	“Recognize short ball; approach with spacing; split-step before volley.”	Orange 02 requires “recognizes short ball”; do coach-fed short-ball recognition with approach+volley sequences. 
Overheads break down (no tracking, poor footwork back)	“Side turn + trophy; drop-step then crossover.”	Green 02/01 overhead movement is explicit; drill service-line overhead with drop-step/crossover and directional targets.
